What is Anti-Money
Laundering (AML)?

Anti-Money Laundering (AML) refers to the set of laws, regulations, and procedures designed to prevent criminals from disguising illegally obtained funds as legitimate income.

Money laundering is the process of making "dirty money" — from crimes like drug trafficking, corruption, fraud, or terrorism — appear "clean" and legally earned.

"The United Nations estimates that between 2% and 5% of global GDP — approximately $800 billion to $2 trillion — is laundered each year."

Financial institutions, banks, insurance companies, real estate agents, and now cryptocurrency exchanges are all legally required to maintain AML compliance programs and report suspicious activities.

In India, the primary AML legislation is the Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA), 2002, enforced by the Enforcement Directorate (ED) and the Financial Intelligence Unit-India (FIU-IND).

Core Concept

The 3 Classic Stages
of Money Laundering

💉
Stage 01

Placement

The first and most risky stage. Illegal cash physically enters the financial system. The criminal tries to deposit large amounts without triggering detection.

Common methods: Smurfing (structuring), cash-intensive businesses, currency exchange, casino chips.

⚠ Example: Depositing ₹9.5 lakh multiple times to avoid ₹10L reporting threshold
🌀
Stage 02

Layering

The most complex stage. Money is moved through multiple transactions, accounts, and jurisdictions to create distance from its criminal origins and confuse investigators.

Common methods: Shell companies, offshore accounts, real estate flipping, cryptocurrency mixing.

⚠ Example: Routing funds through 5 offshore shell companies in 3 countries
✅
Stage 03

Integration

Cleaned money re-enters the legitimate economy. At this stage, it's extremely difficult to distinguish from legally earned income without prior investigation.

Common methods: Luxury real estate, invoicing fraud, stock investments, high-value assets.

⚠ Example: Buying luxury apartments and selling them as "property income"
Compliance Alert

AML Red Flags — What to
Watch Out For

💰

Unusual Large Cash Transactions

Deposits or withdrawals of large amounts of cash without clear business purpose or inconsistent with customer's profile

🔄

Structuring / Smurfing

Breaking large amounts into smaller transactions just below reporting thresholds to avoid detection by authorities

🌐

Unexplained Cross-Border Transfers

Wire transfers to/from high-risk jurisdictions with no clear business reason or relationship to the customer

🏢

Shell Company Involvement

Transactions involving companies with no clear business activity, no employees, or opaque ownership structures

🎰

Casino / Gaming Activity

Buying chips with cash, minimal gambling, then cashing out as "gambling winnings" to legitimize funds

🏠

Real Estate Anomalies

Buying properties at significantly above/below market value, cash purchases, or rapid buying and selling

🔐

Customer Secrecy / Reluctance

Customer refuses to provide KYC documents, is unusually secretive about source of funds or business activities

₿

Crypto Mixing / Tumbling

Using cryptocurrency mixing services to obscure the trail of digital asset transactions across wallets

Compliance Process

KYC — Know Your Customer

01

Customer Identification Program (CIP)

Collect and verify name, date of birth, address, and government ID of every customer before onboarding

02

Customer Due Diligence (CDD)

Understand the nature of the customer's business, assess risk level, and establish expected transaction patterns

03

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD)

For high-risk customers (PEPs, high-value clients, high-risk countries) — deeper investigation into source of funds and wealth

04

Ongoing Monitoring

Continuously monitor transactions against expected behaviour, update KYC records, and file SARs when suspicious activity is detected

05

Record Keeping

Maintain all KYC documents and transaction records for minimum 5 years as required by PMLA and global regulations

Global Framework

AML Laws Around
the World

🇮🇳
India

Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA)

India's primary AML legislation. Enforced by the Enforcement Directorate (ED). Requires reporting entities to maintain KYC records and file Suspicious Transaction Reports with FIU-IND.

2002, Amended 2023
🇺🇸
United States

Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) / USA PATRIOT Act

Requires US financial institutions to assist government agencies in detecting and preventing money laundering. FinCEN oversees compliance and SAR filings.

1970 / 2001
🇪🇺
European Union

EU Anti-Money Laundering Directives (AMLD)

Series of directives (1AMLD to 6AMLD) progressively strengthening AML requirements across EU member states. New AMLA authority to begin in 2025.

6AMLD — 2021
🇬🇧
United Kingdom

Proceeds of Crime Act (POCA)

Comprehensive legislation covering money laundering offences, asset recovery, and the confiscation of criminal proceeds. FCA and NCA enforce compliance.

2002
🌍
Global

FATF 40 Recommendations

The Financial Action Task Force sets international standards for combating money laundering and terrorist financing. 200+ countries follow these recommendations.

Updated 2023
🇸🇬
Singapore

Corruption, Drug Trafficking & Serious Crimes Act

MAS (Monetary Authority of Singapore) enforces strict AML requirements. Singapore is a major financial hub with zero tolerance for AML violations.

1992, Amended 2021
